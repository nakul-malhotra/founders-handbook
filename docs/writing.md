# Writing standard

## The unsummarizable principle

Strive to make the writing unsummarizable: so little fluff remains that if you take any words out — as summaries by definition do — you lose a lot of interesting ideas.

A summary is possible only when the original contains filler. Filler is any sentence, clause, or phrase that:

- restates what the previous sentence already said
- hedges without adding information ("it is worth noting", "in other words", "put another way")
- transitions where no transition is needed ("the same pattern", "as with the above")
- qualifies a point the reader already knows is qualified

If a sentence can be cut without the reader losing a distinct idea, cut it. If a paragraph can be summarised in one sentence without losing anything, that sentence *is* the paragraph and the rest is filler.

## What this does not mean

Density is not compression for its own sake. Short sentences that each say nothing are not better than one long sentence that says something precise. The test is not word count — it is whether every clause carries a specific point the reader would miss if it were gone.

## Applying the standard

When editing, ask of every sentence: *what idea does this sentence contain that no other sentence in this section contains?* If the answer is "none", cut or merge. If the answer is the same as an adjacent sentence, one of them is filler.

Signals of filler:
- opener restates the chapter title or previous chapter's conclusion
- paragraph ends by summarising itself
- transition sentence before a list ("the following should be noted:")
- citation-less sentence that just sets up the next cited sentence

## Why

A reader who can paraphrase the whole thing in two sentences has not been served by the writing — they have been served by their own ability to extract signal from noise. The goal is writing where the paraphrase *is* worse than the original, not interchangeable with it.
